How the State of Indiana Supports High Need Schools

The federal poverty rate for school-age children is declining in Indiana, but this could be due to lawmakers undercounting high-need students. Prior to 2015, Indiana calculated school funding based on students who qualify for free and reduced lunch. Since 2015, Indiana lawmakers decided to calculate school funding by using the number of students who receive food stamps, welfare, or are in foster care.
This decision has cut funding from higher-need schools by 30% since 2015, with new school funding being spread across districts, including wealthier cities such as Carmel and Zionsville.
It is important to note that the values used for these visualizations (IF WE USE MORE) are from 2010 alone.

The Community Eligibility Initiative


The state of Indiana is starting to adopt the community eligibility initiative as of 2019, which allows all students in low-income schools to receive free lunch, regardless of household income. Although this is a step in the right direction, the state of Indiana is likely losing money by providing free lunch to students who are not in need for 180 days out of the year. Why This Matters


Mothers living in low-income neighborhoods are more likely than other mothers to give birth prematurely and have low birthweight babies
Obesity, asthma, and emergency room visits were more common among children in poverty compared with those not in poverty in 2014 (40, 30, and 90 percent more common, respectively).

As impoverished children grow up, they are more likely to engage in risky or unhealthy behaviors, such as smoking cigarettes. They are less likely to complete high school and some studies show an increased risk of teen pregnancy.

Obesity, risk factors for cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and premature mortality have been found to be associated with low socioeconomic status during childhood. Childhood poverty has been linked to difficulty regulating emotions, which is directly linked to the development of mental health conditions later in life, regardless of adult income status.

Living in poverty affects a child's abillity to succeed in school and may impact potential future earnings. One estimate of the social cost of childhood poverty, including lost potential earnings and costs of poor health, totalled $1.03 billion annually in the United States.

Who is Affected?


Racial and ethnic minorities:
Compared with non-Hispanic white and Asian children, disproportionately more black, American Indian, and Hispanic children live below the federal poverty threshold, with more Hispanic children living in poverty than any other racial group in 2017.


Children of single mothers
In 2017, 65.8 percent of poor children were in single-parent households, with 59.5 in single-mother households compared with 8.3 percent in single-father households. 33.5 percent of poor children were in households with married couples.
